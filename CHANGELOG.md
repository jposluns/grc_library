# Changelog

All notable changes to this repository are recorded in this file as one compact entry per change: a `date | version | PR` header followed by a short, plain-language summary a general reader can follow. The full maintainer-grade detail for each change (the Added / Changed / Removed / Fixed / Security / Verification sections) is kept in [`.working/changelog-details/CHANGELOG-detailed.md`](.working/changelog-details/CHANGELOG-detailed.md) and in git history; that mirror is how this project's maintainer tracks the full audit trail. The convention is project-specific; forks may delete `.working/` and adopt their own approach to detailed change tracking. The mechanics are documented in the [`change-tracking` governance rule](dev-security/claude-rules/governance/change-tracking.md).
The format follows [Keep a Changelog](https://keepachangelog.com/en/1.1.0/) loosely; individual document versions follow semantic versioning as defined in [`specification-ingestion.md`](specification-ingestion.md). The library as a whole carries a Calendar Versioning (CalVer) version of the form `YYYY.MM.patch`; see [`specification-master-project.md`](specification-master-project.md) section 4.5.

**Week of 2026-06-29 (PRs #452-#666)**

The dominant workstream was a corpus-wide consistency pass: dozens of policy and standard documents were renumbered to the library's uniform section scheme (cross-references updated, no requirement changed), spelling was harmonized to Canadian orthography (about 2000 changes across roughly 330 documents), mandatory shall wording was converted to must, and the document-lifecycle vocabulary was standardized on Superseded. New adopter instruments were also added, including a privacy-by-design framework, a sanctions and export-control standard, a mergers-and-acquisitions due-diligence procedure, an AI Ethics Review Panel charter, and several fill-in templates (transfer-impact assessment, legitimate-interest assessment, and a breach-regulator register).

**Week of 2026-06-22 (PRs #187-#451)**

The defining effort was a comprehensive expansion of the compliance alignment matrix, which grew domain by domain (architecture, risk, resilience, operations, compliance, governance, security, dev-security, supply-chain, privacy, and AI) from a curated subset toward the full document set, with many mis-fitting control codes corrected against their source catalogues. Fabricated or inaccurate legal content was corrected, notably Canada's never-enacted CPPA relisted as the in-force PIPEDA across twenty-nine documents and an invented 72-hour PIPEDA breach deadline removed, while new privacy instruments (joint-controller, data-processing-agreement, EU-representative, and China cross-border-transfer material) and substantial internal audit-tooling and AI-assistant governance work were added.
