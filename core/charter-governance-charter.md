## Purpose

This Charter establishes the governance framework, authority model, and accountability structure that guide the development, approval, and oversight of governance instruments across the enterprise. Governance instruments include frameworks, policies, standards, procedures, guidelines, registers, and matrices.

The Charter ensures alignment with organizational strategy, global regulatory obligations, and internationally recognized standards including ISO, NIST, COBIT 2025, and Cloud Security Alliance guidance. It defines the principles and structures that support consistent, transparent, and effective governance outcomes.

## Scope

This Charter applies to all business units, subsidiaries, departments, and third-party engagements under organizational oversight. It governs:

1. The creation, maintenance, and retirement of governance instruments.  
2. The roles and responsibilities of contributors, reviewers, approvers, and custodians.  
3. Governance of meta-governance elements such as compliance mappings, regulatory references, and digital-trust performance indicators.  
4. Integration of cross-functional requirements spanning information security, technology, privacy, AI governance, sustainability, compliance, and enterprise risk.  
5. Trade-security and supply-chain governance based on global frameworks including WCO SAFE, ISO 28000, BASC, PIP, CTPAT, AEO, and internationally recognized equivalents.

## Objectives

1. Establish a unified, transparent governance authority model.  
2. Ensure governance instruments conform to defined quality, structure, and alignment requirements.  
3. Promote consistent lifecycle management for all governance artefacts.  
4. Strengthen accountability for oversight, change management, and compliance.  
5. Support continuous improvement and digital-trust outcomes.

## Governance and Accountability

### Governance Principles

The governance model is built on the following principles:

1. Integrity: Governance decisions and conduct reflect legal, ethical, and professional standards.  
2. Transparency: Governance changes and decisions are communicated consistently and documented.  
3. Continuous Improvement: Governance practices are updated to reflect changes in risk, technology, and regulatory requirements.  
4. Digital Trust: Governance supports transparency, accountability, resilience, and ethical use of technology.

### Governance Hierarchy

1. Governance Charter: Defines authority, structure, and approval processes.  
2. Frameworks: Provide strategic structure and integration of governance domains.  
3. Policies: Establish executive intent and mandatory governance principles.  
4. Standards: Define measurable control requirements and baselines.  
5. Procedures: Specify operational methods for implementing standards.  
6. Guidelines: Offer advisory methods where flexibility is permitted.  
7. Registers and Matrices: Consolidate mappings, metrics, inventories, and assurance evidence.

### Governance Structure and Oversight

1. Chief Executive Officer: Maintains ultimate accountability for governance effectiveness.  
2. Chief Governance Officer: Oversees the GRC library and governance architecture.  
3. Chief Information Security Officer: Oversees information-security governance and resilience.  
4. Chief Compliance Officer: Leads compliance governance and regulatory monitoring.  
5. Chief Legal Officer: Provides legal oversight and regulatory interpretation.  
6. Chief Risk Officer: Oversees enterprise-risk governance, appetite, and monitoring.  
7. Chief Technology Officer: Oversees technology architecture and innovation governance.  
8. Chief Sustainability Officer: Integrates ESG and responsible-technology requirements.  
9. Chief Human Resources Officer: Oversees workforce governance, ethics, and training.  
10. Enterprise Risk Committee: Provides senior oversight of enterprise risk, audit results, and compliance outcomes.

## Policy and Control Statements

1. Governance artefacts must support organizational strategy, regulatory requirements, and recognized frameworks.  
2. All governance artefacts must declare parent documents, related frameworks, and dependent documents.  
3. Change management must be applied consistently for creation, review, update, and retirement of governance artefacts.  
4. All approved governance artefacts must be stored in the authoritative GRC repository.  
5. Meta-governance artefacts including compliance mappings, regulatory registers, and digital-trust indicators must be maintained as separate documents and referenced from related governance instruments.  
6. Version control must ensure clear traceability, archival of superseded versions, and transparent revision history (outside the CC0 document).  
7. Governance instruments must incorporate globally recognized supply-chain and trade-security requirements where applicable.

## Roles and Responsibilities

1. Governance Owners: Maintain accuracy, applicability, and currency of assigned documents.  
2. Approving Authorities: Approve governance artefacts in alignment with defined approval workflows.  
3. Contributors and Reviewers: Ensure clarity, alignment, and adherence to governance standards during development.  
4. Enterprise Risk Committee: Receives consolidated governance and compliance reporting.  
5. All Personnel: Must follow approved governance instruments and report gaps, risks, or improvement opportunities.

## Monitoring, Metrics, and Reporting

1. Governance effectiveness is monitored through audits, maturity assessments, and digital-trust metrics.  
2. Risk, compliance, and performance indicators are reviewed regularly by oversight committees.  
3. Deviations or exceptions must be documented and processed through the defined exception-management process.

## Continuous Improvement

1. Governance instruments are reviewed annually or upon significant regulatory, technological, or strategic changes.  
2. Lessons learned, audit findings, and incident data inform updates to governance instruments.  
3. Non-substantive editorial updates may be made without re-approval but must be logged for audit traceability.
